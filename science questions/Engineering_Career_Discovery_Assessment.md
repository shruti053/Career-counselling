# Engineering Self-Realization Assessment
## For Students of Classes 10–12

**Purpose:** This assessment helps you discover how you naturally think, approach problems, and connect ideas. It is designed to create self-awareness about whether engineering thinking aligns with your natural style. There are no "right" or "wrong" answers in the traditional sense — every choice reveals something meaningful about how your mind works.

**Instructions:** Read each question carefully. Choose the option that feels most natural to you. Take your time. Reflect on why you chose what you did.

**Total Questions: 40**

## SECTION A — Physics Thinking (Questions 1-6)

**Question 1**  
**Category:** Physics Thinking  

A child throws a ball toward a balcony on the second floor of a building. The ball goes up in the air and then comes down before reaching the balcony. Which path best describes the movement of the ball as you visualize it?

A. It moves in a perfectly straight line from the child's hand to the balcony.  
B. It follows a smooth curved path that rises and then falls like an arc.  
C. It moves in small zigzags due to slight changes in direction.  
D. It moves in a circular loop before settling on the balcony.  

**Correct Answer:** B  

**Why This Question Matters:** This reveals whether you intuitively understand the combined effects of initial velocity and gravity without needing formulas.  

**Insight Being Measured:** Spatial Visualization and Intuition about Motion

## How to Use This Assessment

After completing all 40 questions:

1. Review your chosen options across sections.
2. Notice patterns in your choices (e.g., do you prefer optimization, exploration, or quick decisions?).
3. Reflect on the "Insight Being Measured" for questions where your choice surprised you.
4. Consider: Does the way you think match the thinking style often found in engineering?

This assessment is meant to spark self-reflection. Discuss your responses with a teacher, parent, or counselor for deeper insights.

**Remember:** Engineering is not just about being good at math or science. It is about how you approach problems, handle uncertainty, and enjoy the process of building solutions.
